Abstract

Inherent shortcomings like data scarcity and compounded sub-tasks hinder the improvement of robot policies depending on training phases. Particularly, long-horizon tasks amplify these challenges. While post-training revision shows promise, feedback and rules from human and foundation models typically rely on error detection or constraints fitting, which lack continuous, step-wise updates and fail to generalize to other tasks. Additionally, straightforward error recovery often leads to impractical execution time. In this paper, we propose Step-wise Self Correction by Weak Alignment (SeCoWA), a novel approach that offers a quantitatively measured, step-wise self-correction without trace-back recovery or human intervention during deployment. Central to SeCoWA are two core concepts: the Gradients Positive Alignment Lemma and Expectation Divergence Decreasing by Weak Supervisor Alignment Scheme, for which we provide theoretical proof of delivering improvements in task success rates, especially for long-horizon tasks. This improvement arises from continuous and accumulated alignments with progress predictions from foundation models, which serve as weak supervisors. We observe substantial success rate increases in all tasks across 22 single tasks and four composite tasks on the RoboCasa platform, outperforming baseline models. Our algorithm boosts success rates from zero to nonzero in several tasks. Our proposed approach is modular and adaptable, allowing seamless integration into our robot learning model to enhance success rates, and providing a practical method for leveraging foundation models in complex decision-making with self-improvement.


Key Ideas and Contributions


1) A self-correcting mechanism for improving success rates step-wisely during task execution using surrogate tasks: We give theoretical proofs and practical experiments to introduce a novel method that enhances robot policy action predictions at test time by optimizing a surrogate task, even when the policy lacks knowledge of optimal actions. This method doesn't require human feedback and avoids physical tracebacks.
2) Demonstration of the effectiveness of using foundation model for continuous quantitative updates in robot policy during test time, even with weak supervision: We leverage vision and language foundation models and train \textit{SeCoWA} with human expert labels, enabling it to give delicate and step-wise feedback for robot execution by predicting the task's completion percentage at each time step.
3) A practical, comprehensive and plugin-able implementation for applying SeCOWA to other robot learning tasks: We provide the open-source model building based on foundation models, guidelines for extending current model with SeCOWA ability, and the processes for self-correction during test phases. These can be easily transferred and applied to other related tasks.
